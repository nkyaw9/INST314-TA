Statistic Made Easy

Treatment Group VS. Control Group

  • Treatment Group: expose to the new variable
  • Control Group: not exposed to the new variable
    • credit to OpenIntro Statistics

Note: You will often encounter these types of tables, study them.

Variables

  • There are two types of variables: Categorical vs. Numerical
    • Categorical: You cannot put a number on them (lables or names)
      • Ordinal: Ordered categories (beginner, intermediate, advance)
      • Nominal: Named (female and male)
    • Numerical: You can put a number on them (# numbers)
      • Continuous: Uncountable number of values (12.241251...)
      • Discrete: Countable numbers (1, 2, 3,4)

Independent Variable Vs. Dependent Variable

  • Independent (explanatory): Cause
  • Dependent (response): Effect
    • credit to OpenIntro Statistics
  • Think of rise over run. Change in y-axis depends on the change in x-axis.

Association

  • You identify if two variables are correlated by their correlation coffiecnt
  • Range: -1 ≤ x ≤ 1
  • 0 means there is no corrlelation
  • closer it gets to -1 or 1, the more the variables are negatively or positvely correlated
  • Its either correlated or not, never both
  • ASSOCIATION ≠ CAUSATION
  • Even if two variables have a relationship, doesn't mean its causation as their might be other cofounding variables at play
    • credit to SimplyPsychology

Population, Parameter, Sample, Statistic

  • Note: You need to be able to define these terms
  • A population refers to ALL
  • A sample refers to a subset of the population
  • A parameter is a number that descibes the population
  • A statistic is a number that describes the sample
  • PP for population parameter & SS for sample statistic
  • Watch this video: Statistics Introduction - Population, Sample, Parameter, Statistic by Medicosis Perfectionalis

Study Design Observational Study

  • Observational Studies: Observing without interfering with the environment
    • Cohort (prospective): follows people forward to see what happens in the future
      • Retrospective: uses existing records/data to look backward at what already happened
    • Cross-Sectional: what is happening right now (present)
    • Case-Control: what happened in the past
    • Note: Tries to establish correlation
  • Experimental Studies: In a controlled setting
    • Note: Tries to establish correlation

Key Types of Summary Statistics

  • Summary Statistic: One number that summarizes a lot of data
  • Central Tendency (The center)
    • Mean: The average.
    • Median: The literal middle value.
    • Mode: The most frequent value.
  • Dispersion (The Spread)
    • Range: The diffence between the highest and lowest values.
    • Standard Deviation: Shows how much the data varies from the average.
    • Interquartile Range (IQR): Shows the spread of the middle 50% of the data.
  • Shape: Describes how data is distributed
    • Skewness
    • Symmetric
